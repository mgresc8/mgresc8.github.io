About

This portfolio documents a semester-long inquiry into high-functioning autism as a cultural category — tracing how clinical frameworks, educational systems, and social norms define, limit, and sometimes enable neurological difference.

The work moves from an initial analytical draft through a research proposal, annotated bibliography, and PechaKucha presentation, arriving at a final argumentative essay that reframes disability not as a fixed individual condition, but as something an environment can produce.

High-Functioning Autism: Intelligence or Disability?

An examination of the tension embedded in the label "high-functioning autism," where intelligence implies competence but autism is still read as disability. Drawing on clinical literature, neurodiversity research, and personal experience, this draft argues autistic cognition represents difference rather than deficiency — and that disability reveals itself in environments that misvalue certain ways of thinking.

Abstract, Proposal & Annotated Bibliography

A formal research proposal investigating the high-functioning autism paradox across three areas: clinical definitions, educational structures, and cultural values. The proposal argues the label is not a fixed medical truth but a category shaped by social norms. 04 Spring 2026
PechaKucha Presentation

Internship Proposal: Rethinking High-Functioning Autism in Educational Spaces

A PechaKucha-format slide deck proposing an internship with a school, education nonprofit, or disability advocacy organization to study how classroom design shapes the experience of high-functioning autism. The presentation identifies the core problem — schools reward neurotypical behaviors like verbal speed, group discussion, and eye contact while overlooking pattern recognition and deep focus — and proposes observing classrooms, interviewing students and teachers, and redesigning participation structures from the ground up. Core thesis: Change the room, not the student.

05 May 13, 2026
Final Essay

Disability as an Environment: High-Functioning Autism and the Classroom

The capstone essay argues that disability is not solely located inside an individual — it is something an environment produces. Schools claim to measure intelligence but often measure conformity: verbal speed, eye contact, group participation, fast task-switching. Drawing on disability studies, neurodiversity theory, cognitive research, and lived experience, the essay reframes accommodation not as lowering the standard but as redesigning the path to it. Disability is not only a condition; it is also a design outcome.
